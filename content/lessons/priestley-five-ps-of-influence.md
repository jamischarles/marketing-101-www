# The Five Ps of a Key Person of Influence

Daniel Priestley's argument in *Key Person of Influence* is that every industry has a small number of people who get the best opportunities, the best deals, the best partnerships, and the best clients — not because they're the most technically skilled, but because they've made themselves **visible and credible** in their niche. He calls these people Key People of Influence (KPIs), and he claims the path to becoming one is the same five steps for everyone.

> "In every industry, there's a small number of people — the Key People of Influence — who get the best opportunities… The good news is that the path to becoming one is explicit, learnable, and repeatable."
> *— Daniel Priestley, Key Person of Influence*

The five steps — the **Five Ps** — are: **Pitch, Publish, Product, Profile, Partnerships**. Priestley is adamant that they must be done **in order**, because each step builds the foundation for the next.

## 1. Pitch

Before anything else, you need a **clear, compelling answer** to "what do you do?" Most founders give a 40-second rambling answer. Priestley wants a 7-second answer that is:

- Specific about who you help
- Specific about what problem you solve
- Specific about the result you produce
- Interesting enough that the listener wants to hear more

Example of weak: *"I run a SaaS company that does analytics."*
Example of strong: *"I help Series-B SaaS founders stop guessing what their customers will churn on — we catch the 3-4 warning signals two months out."*

The pitch is the atomic unit of everything else. Your website copy, your cold email, your podcast intros, your investor decks, your landing pages — they're all variants of the pitch. If the pitch is weak, every downstream asset is weak. If the pitch is sharp, every downstream asset inherits the sharpness.

This is the Priestley equivalent of Hormozi's "dream outcome" in the Value Equation — see `hormozi-grand-slam-offer`. Both authors agree: if you can't say **what you do** with specificity and confidence, your marketing won't work no matter how much you spend.

## 2. Publish

Once you have the pitch, you need to put your thinking into the world in a form that can be **read, shared, and referenced**. Priestley's strong preference: write a book, or at minimum publish a substantial piece of anchor content (long-form article, whitepaper, public framework) that can serve as a credibility asset.

Why publishing matters:

- It forces you to organize your thinking into a coherent worldview.
- It gives other people a way to refer to you ("have you read her book on X?") which compounds into authority.
- It removes the need for you to re-explain your framework in every conversation.
- It's the ultimate scalable pre-sale: prospects who read your book arrive at sales calls already convinced.

This is the layer of the Five Ps that most founders skip because it feels disproportionately slow. Priestley's counter: **publishing is a one-time investment that pays for years**. A book or flagship essay you write this quarter will still be generating leads, speaking invitations, and partnerships five years from now.

## 3. Product

Priestley means **productized** — your expertise packaged into a discrete, repeatable thing with a clear price. Not "we do consulting, call us for a quote." More like: "The Pre-Launch Audit, 2 weeks, $15K, here's exactly what you get."

Productization does three things simultaneously:

1. **Removes friction from buying** (customers can read the product page and decide without a sales call)
2. **Enables scale** (you can deliver the same product repeatedly and hire people into the delivery)
3. **Creates a ladder** — once you have one product, you can build others: an entry-level offer, a mid-tier, a premium. See `priestley-ecosystem-of-products` (planned future lesson) and Hormozi's value ladder thinking.

A common founder mistake: skipping straight from Pitch to running paid ads, without a clearly-productized thing to sell. The ads drive traffic; the traffic hits a vague "learn more / book a call" page; conversions die.

## 4. Profile

Profile = your public presence across the places where your ideal customers already pay attention. Podcasts (as a guest), conferences (as a speaker), trade press (as a source), LinkedIn, YouTube, specialist Twitter/X, niche newsletters.

Priestley's specific prescription: **don't try to build profile broadly**. Pick the 5-10 venues where your niche actually is and dominate those. Being the 100th-most-known person in a mega-niche is worthless. Being the 3rd-most-known person in a narrow niche is a business.

Profile is the channel that compounds. Early on, you chase opportunities. Once profile crosses a threshold, opportunities chase you — this is the shift Priestley is trying to manufacture. Cross-links to Weinberg's channels 18 (speaking engagements) and 19 (community building), see `traction-19-channels`.

## 5. Partnerships

The final step is strategic partnerships: joint ventures, affiliate arrangements, bundled offerings, co-created products, referral relationships with complementary experts. Partnerships are **only possible after** the first four Ps are in place, because potential partners evaluate you based on Pitch (can I describe what you do?), Publish (is there a credibility asset I can point to?), Product (is there a thing I can actually sell or refer people to?), and Profile (will being associated with you help or hurt me?).

Founders who try to do partnerships without the first four Ps usually experience partnerships as ghosted emails and no follow-through. Founders who do them in order find that partners approach them.

## The order is the point

The Five Ps are often quoted as a list, but the **order is the lesson**. You can't run paid ads productively without a Pitch. You can't publish a book without knowing your Pitch. You can't productize without knowing what you've been publishing about. You can't build Profile without a Product to point people to. And you can't do Partnerships until everything else exists. Skipping steps produces expensive failure.

## Checklist

- [ ] Write your 7-second pitch. Test it on 5 people. Rewrite until they repeat it back correctly.
- [ ] Commit to a publishing project (book, flagship essay, public framework) with a deadline
- [ ] Turn your expertise into **one** productized offer with a fixed scope, price, and deliverables
- [ ] Identify the 5-10 venues where your niche lives. Make appearing in them your Critical Path
- [ ] Only pursue partnerships after the first four Ps are in place
