# The 19 Traction Channels

The core claim of *Traction* is that every startup, no matter what it sells, acquires customers through some combination of 19 channels. Not 50. Not 5. Nineteen.

The point isn't memorization — it's **forcing yourself to consider all 19** before committing to one. When you run the Bullseye Framework (see `traction-bullseye-framework`), this is the checklist you work through.

> "Most founders only consider a couple of traction channels seriously… which is why they fail at traction."
> *— Weinberg & Mares, Traction, Ch. 4*

Each channel below has a one-line thesis, when it tends to work, and — where applicable — a cross-link to the tactical skill on this site that covers execution.

## The 19 channels

1. **Viral Marketing** — Growth where each user brings in more users (K-factor > 1). Works for products with inherent network effects or social sharing built into the core loop. → `referral-program`

2. **Public Relations (PR)** — Coverage in traditional press (tech blogs, mainstream media). Works when you have a genuine story hook: new launch, contrarian data, a founder narrative.

3. **Unconventional PR** — Stunts, publicity hacks, customer appreciation plays that drive word-of-mouth. Think Dollar Shave Club's launch video. Riskier but cheaper than traditional PR.

4. **Search Engine Marketing (SEM)** — Paid search ads (Google Ads primarily). Works when prospects already know they want what you sell and are searching for it. → `paid-ads`

5. **Social & Display Ads** — Paid ads on Meta, LinkedIn, TikTok, Reddit, display networks. Best for visual products and demand-generation rather than capturing existing intent. → `paid-ads`, `ad-creative`

6. **Offline Ads** — TV, radio, billboards, direct mail, magazines. Viable for consumer products at scale or hyper-targeted B2B markets. Most underrated channel on the list for mature startups.

7. **Search Engine Optimization (SEO)** — Organic search traffic. Slow to ramp, high leverage once it works. Best for products where users have a defined search intent. → `seo-audit`, `ai-seo`, `programmatic-seo`

8. **Content Marketing** — Blog, podcast, YouTube, newsletter. The long game. Works when you can consistently publish something genuinely useful for years. → `content-strategy`, `social-content`

9. **Email Marketing** — Nurture sequences, newsletters, lifecycle campaigns. Best framed as retention and conversion, not acquisition — though cold email (#19-ish) is technically a variant. → `email-sequence`, `cold-email`

10. **Engineering as Marketing** — Build a free tool, calculator, or useful utility that attracts your ideal customer. Classic examples: HubSpot's Website Grader, Nerdwallet's calculators. → `free-tool-strategy`

11. **Targeting Blogs** — Sponsor, guest post on, or get covered by niche blogs your audience already reads. Easier than big-press PR; higher conversion because of audience fit.

12. **Business Development (BD)** — Strategic partnerships, distribution deals, revenue-share arrangements with complementary companies. High-leverage but slow and relationship-intensive.

13. **Sales** — Direct human outreach and pipeline. Essential for deals above a certain ACV threshold. → `sales-enablement`, `cold-email`, `revops`

14. **Affiliate Programs** — Third parties promote you for a commission. Low overhead, but requires a clear payout structure and a product with a long enough payback window.

15. **Existing Platforms** — Build on top of an existing audience (App Store, Shopify App Store, Chrome Web Store, Zapier, Slack Marketplace). Cheap distribution for new entrants; risky long-term because the platform owns the relationship.

16. **Trade Shows** — Industry events with exhibit halls. Still extremely effective for B2B in legacy industries. Underused by software-native founders because they dismiss it.

17. **Offline Events** — Meetups, conferences you host, dinners, workshops. Build community and pipeline simultaneously. → `community-marketing`

18. **Speaking Engagements** — Keynotes, panels, podcast guest appearances. Positions the founder as a category expert; drives both pipeline and recruiting.

19. **Community Building** — Invest in a community of users or adjacent professionals (forum, Discord, Slack group, subreddit). Slow to seed, deeply compounding. → `community-marketing`

## How to use this list

1. Start a fresh document. Write down **all 19**. Don't skip any.
2. For each, write one sentence describing a concrete experiment you could actually run in your business in the next 30 days.
3. Circle the 3 that seem most promising for your stage.
4. Go run them in parallel. See `traction-bullseye-framework` for the testing protocol.

## Layered principle: Hormozi's Core Four is a simpler remix

Alex Hormozi's *$100M Leads* boils all lead-generation down to four sources: **warm outreach, cold outreach, posting content, paid ads**. This is a useful second-pass filter — if you find the 19 channels overwhelming, collapse them into Hormozi's four quadrants (one-to-one vs one-to-many × warm vs cold) and pick the quadrant that fits your founder-strength and business model. See `hormozi-core-four-leads`.

## Layered principle: Priestley's Five Ps pick the channels for you

Daniel Priestley's *Key Person of Influence* argues that the most durable channels are the ones that build the founder's personal brand: **pitch, publish, product, profile, partnerships**. When your name becomes the channel, speaking engagements (#18), content marketing (#8), and community building (#19) compound far faster. See `priestley-five-ps-of-influence`.
