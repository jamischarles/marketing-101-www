# The Critical Path and the 50/50 Rule

Most startups don't die of too few ideas — they die of too many. *Traction* has two opinionated tools for fixing this: **the Critical Path** and **the 50/50 Rule**. Together they force founders into the uncomfortable question of what they should **not** work on.

> "Pursuing traction in parallel with product development has taken on the moniker of 'traction-driven development.' The 50% rule: spend 50% of your time on product and 50% on traction."
> *— Weinberg & Mares, Traction, on founder time allocation*

## The 50/50 rule

The idea is simple and unpopular: **founders should spend half their time on traction from day one**, not "after the product is ready."

Why founders resist this:

- Building feels productive and measurable. Marketing feels uncomfortable and slow.
- "We'll focus on growth after launch" is a more comfortable story than "We don't know how we'll get customers, and we should find out now."
- Engineering founders often believe, subconsciously, that a great product markets itself.

Why Weinberg and Mares insist on 50/50:

1. **Traction experiments take time to produce data.** If you start them on launch day, you'll still be running your first tests three months later. Start earlier.
2. **Traction work reveals product gaps.** The fastest way to find out what your market actually wants is to try to sell it. Sales conversations, ad copy A/B tests, and cold outreach all generate product feedback that building-in-a-cave cannot.
3. **Channel experiments have lead times.** SEO takes 6 months to show signal. Content takes 12. If you wait until product is "ready," you're adding a year to your zero-to-one timeline.

The practical implementation: block off half your calendar for traction work — customer research, channel tests, writing, outreach, funnel instrumentation — and protect it with the same rigor you protect engineering time.

## The Critical Path

The Critical Path is the smallest set of actions that leads to your next traction milestone. You define:

1. **A specific, measurable traction goal.** Not "grow the business." Something like "1,000 paying customers," "$10K MRR," "500 weekly active teams." The goal must have a number and a deadline.
2. **The shortest, clearest path to that goal.** Which channel? Which funnel fix? Which partnership?
3. **Everything else gets cut or deferred.**

The Critical Path is ruthless because it requires saying no to obviously-good ideas that aren't on the path. A redesign of your pricing page might lift conversion 10% — but if your Critical Path is "reach 1,000 customers through cold outbound," the pricing page isn't on the path, and doing it delays the path.

## The weekly Critical Path review

Weinberg recommends a weekly ritual:

1. **Write down the traction goal and deadline** at the top of the page. No changes mid-week.
2. **List everything you worked on this week.**
3. **For each item, ask: did this move me toward the goal?** Be honest. Most weeks, half your work will fail this test.
4. **For next week, plan only work that's on the Critical Path.** Park everything else in a "someday" list.
5. **Repeat.**

The discipline isn't the planning — it's the honesty. Founders who do this for a month are invariably shocked at how much of their work was not on the path.

## Interaction with the Bullseye Framework

The Critical Path tells you **which channel you're committed to**. The Bullseye Framework (see `traction-bullseye-framework`) tells you **how to pick that channel**. They're complementary:

- Use Bullseye to decide which 3 channels to test and which one becomes the winner.
- Use the Critical Path to stay committed to the winner and refuse distractions.

The failure mode without Critical Path thinking: you pick a channel via Bullseye, make partial progress, get excited about a shiny new idea, and pivot before the first channel has had a chance to compound.

## Layered principle: Hormozi's "More, Better, New" diagnostic

When the Critical Path stalls — you're working the channel but growth has flattened — Hormozi has a three-part diagnostic: **do more of what's working, make it better, or try something new** (in that order). The order matters. Most founders jump to "new" too fast because it's more exciting than optimizing what already works. The Critical Path gives you permission to stay in the "more" and "better" phases longer than feels comfortable.

## Layered principle: Priestley's productized focus

Daniel Priestley's *Key Person of Influence* argues that the highest-leverage founders **productize one core thing** — a signature framework, book, keynote, or assessment — and let that one product drive everything else: pitches, partnerships, profile, publishing. The Priestley angle on the Critical Path: your path is clearer when your business has one signature product, not five parallel initiatives. See `priestley-five-ps-of-influence`.

## Checklist

- [ ] Write down one specific traction goal with a number and deadline
- [ ] Identify the single channel (via Bullseye) that will get you there
- [ ] Write the shortest-path sequence of work to reach the goal
- [ ] Audit your current week: what % of your time went to work on the Critical Path?
- [ ] Are you actually spending ~50% on traction vs product? Track it for 2 weeks.
- [ ] Before any new initiative, ask: "Is this on the Critical Path, or a distraction?"
