# The Bullseye Framework

Most founders fail at traction not because they pick the wrong channel, but because they never systematically **considered** every channel in the first place. They default to whatever feels familiar — usually SEO, content, or social ads — and ignore 16 other viable paths to customers.

Weinberg and Mares wrote *Traction* to fix that. The Bullseye Framework is their answer: a repeatable process for finding the single channel that will unlock the next order of magnitude of growth.

> "Startups get traction through 19 different channels. Many successful startups experimented with multiple channels until they found one that worked."
> *— Weinberg & Mares, Traction, Ch. 3*

## The three rings

Picture a dartboard. Three concentric rings.

**Outer ring — What's possible.** Brainstorm every channel, including the ones you think won't work. The goal here is **coverage**, not selection. If you dismiss a channel before writing it down, you've already failed at the framework. For each of the 19 channels, write down the single best idea you have for how you'd use it, even if you think it's a long shot.

**Middle ring — What's probable.** From the outer ring, pick the 3 channels that seem most promising. These become your testing candidates. The discipline: you run all 3 tests **in parallel**, not sequentially. Sequential testing is how founders waste a year on SEO before discovering their customers actually respond to cold outreach.

**Inner ring — What's working.** After the tests, exactly one channel usually emerges as the winner. That becomes the inner ring. Now focus everything on extracting maximum traction from it until it stops working.

## Why parallel testing matters

Each test in the middle ring should be designed to answer three questions cheaply:

1. **Roughly how much does it cost to acquire a customer through this channel?**
2. **Roughly how many customers are available through this channel?**
3. **Are the customers I'm getting through this channel the ones I want right now?**

The tests aren't supposed to produce real growth. They're supposed to produce **data** — enough to decide which channel to commit to. Weinberg is explicit that running 3 sloppy tests in parallel beats running 1 careful test sequentially, because parallel tests let you compare relative performance rather than judging a channel in isolation.

## The inner-ring trap

Once you find a working channel, the natural instinct is to ride it forever. Don't. Channels decay. What worked at 100 customers will break at 10,000. When growth plateaus in the inner ring, you don't go back to the middle ring you already tested — you start the Bullseye over from the outer ring, because your stage, audience, and competitive landscape have all changed.

## How this interacts with the rest of your marketing

The Bullseye Framework is **meta** — it sits above all the tactical skills in this library. You use Bullseye to pick the channel; then you use the channel-specific skill (`paid-ads`, `seo-audit`, `referral-program`, `free-tool-strategy`, etc.) to execute.

A common mistake: founders read a CRO or SEO guide, get excited, and skip the Bullseye step entirely. Result: they're optimizing a channel that was never going to be their biggest lever.

## Checklist

- [ ] Write down one concrete idea for all 19 traction channels (see `traction-19-channels`)
- [ ] Circle the 3 most promising
- [ ] For each of the 3, define: cost per acquisition target, sample size, time budget, kill criteria
- [ ] Run the 3 tests in parallel, not sequentially
- [ ] Compare results; commit to the winner
- [ ] When growth plateaus, restart from the outer ring
